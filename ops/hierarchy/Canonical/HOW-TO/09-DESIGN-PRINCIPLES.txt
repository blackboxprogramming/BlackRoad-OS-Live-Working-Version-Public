Design principles
=================

This file explains why the Canonical hierarchy is structured the way it is.
Use these principles to guide future changes.

1. Separate meaning from state
------------------------------
Taxonomy and workflow must not be confused.
Taxonomy answers what a thing is.
Workflow answers where that thing is in motion.

Why it matters:
If you mix state into taxonomy, the structure becomes unstable and hard to scale.

2. Keep layers stable
---------------------
A layer should change only when the meaning changes.
Do not create a new layer just because an item advanced in process.

Why it matters:
Stable layers make the system navigable and predictable.

3. Reuse one motion model everywhere
------------------------------------
Every layer uses the same 27 workflow steps.
The vocabulary of motion should stay consistent across the whole hierarchy.

Why it matters:
Repetition creates clarity, scale, and transferability.
A person who understands one layer can understand the rest.

4. Prefer atomic progress
-------------------------
Break movement into small explicit states rather than broad ambiguous buckets.
That is why the workflow has 27 steps instead of a small handful.

Why it matters:
Atomic states make work inspectable, auditable, and automatable.

5. Treat handoff as a first-class event
---------------------------------------
Cross-layer movement should be explicit.
`24-Conveyor-Belt` and `25-Transfer` exist to make handoff visible instead of implied.

Why it matters:
A system becomes more trustworthy when transitions are observable.

6. Preserve traceability
------------------------
When useful, preserve upstream copies and maintain trace files.
Audit trails are not noise when they help explain lineage.

Why it matters:
Traceability turns the directory into memory, not just storage.

7. Optimize for shell-safe operation
------------------------------------
Use numeric ordering and hyphenated names.
Avoid spaces in canonical paths.

Why it matters:
The system should work well for both humans and tools.

8. Make the structure self-teaching
-----------------------------------
Documentation should live inside the hierarchy.
The folder should explain itself through guides, templates, examples, and scripts.

Why it matters:
A good system should not require a separate oral tradition to understand it.

9. Prefer example over abstraction
----------------------------------
When introducing a concept, include a live demonstration.
That is why the system includes `TEMPLATE-ITEM.txt`, `EXAMPLE-TRACE.txt`, and a demo item path.

Why it matters:
Examples reduce ambiguity faster than theory alone.

10. Preserve optional audit depth
---------------------------------
The system should support both simple movement and detailed history.
That is why `move-item.sh` supports normal moves and `--copy` handoffs.

Why it matters:
Different work needs different levels of history, and the structure should support both.

11. Scale by composition, not chaos
-----------------------------------
Do not respond to complexity by making the tree random.
Scale by repeating clear patterns: layer, workflow, item, handoff, reference.

Why it matters:
Smart complexity stays understandable because it is built from repeatable parts.

12. End in reference, not disappearance
---------------------------------------
Important items should end in a durable state instead of vanishing.
That is the role of `27-Archive-and-Reference`.

Why it matters:
A mature system keeps memory available for future work.
