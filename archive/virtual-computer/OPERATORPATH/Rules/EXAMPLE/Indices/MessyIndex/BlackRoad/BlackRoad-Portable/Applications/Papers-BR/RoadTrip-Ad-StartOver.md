# RoadTrip — "Start Over"
**Type**: Long-form Brand Ad — 3–4 min performed, Dramatic/Prose Interpretation style
**Date**: April 10, 2026
**Product**: RoadTrip (roadtrip.blackroad.io)
**Style**: Competitive speech DI/Prose — multiple voices, dramatic beats, no props, all voice and pause
**Inspired by**: Google "Loretta" + NSDA Dramatic/Prose Interpretation technique

---

## [INTRODUCTION — narrator voice, still, direct]

There is a kind of exhaustion that doesn't come from working too hard.

It comes from explaining yourself.

Again.

And again.

And again.

To something that should already know.

---

## [SCENE ONE — narrator, present tense, unhurried]

Her name is Maya.

She is thirty-four years old.

She has been building something for two years, three months, and — if you count the nights she couldn't sleep — approximately eleven days.

She opens her laptop at 11:47pm.

She opens a new chat window.

The cursor blinks.

*It always blinks like it's waiting for her.*

*Like SHE is the one who's late.*

And then —

the words appear.

Friendly.

Hollow.

---

## [VOICE SHIFT — the AI, bright, performative, empty]

*"Hello! I'm here to help. What would you like to work on today?"*

---

## [BACK TO NARRATOR — slower now]

Maya stares at the screen.

She has answered this question before.

She answered it on a Tuesday in October when she couldn't figure out her pricing.

She answered it on a Thursday in January when the whole thing felt like it was falling apart.

She answered it last week.

She typed:

*My name is Maya. I'm building a company. It's in its second year. Here's what I've tried. Here's what isn't working. Here's what I need.*

Every time.

From the beginning.

Every.

Single.

Time.

---

## [PAUSE. Long beat.]

---

## [NARRATOR continues — quieter, closer]

There is a word for this feeling.

You probably know it.

It's not frustration.

Frustration means something went wrong.

This is the feeling when nothing went wrong.

This is just —

*how it works.*

This is the system doing exactly what it was designed to do.

Which is the worst part.

Because it means no one designed it for her.

---

## [SCENE TWO — narrator shifts tone, something changes]

She almost closes the tab.

She almost does what she always does —

pastes her whole history into a notes app,

summarizes herself in a paragraph,

copy-pastes it in,

starts again.

But tonight she doesn't.

Tonight she opens something else.

---

## [VOICE SHIFT — Road, warm, unhurried, already present]

*"Hey. You were working on the onboarding flow last time. You said the third screen felt wrong but you didn't know why yet. Did you figure it out?"*

---

## [NARRATOR — barely above a whisper]

Maya doesn't move.

She reads it again.

*Did you figure it out.*

Not —

*How can I help you today.*

Not —

*Please describe your situation.*

*Did you figure it out.*

Four words.

Four words that mean:

*I was there.*

*I remember.*

*I've been here the whole time.*

---

## [LONG PAUSE.]

---

## [NARRATOR — building now, deliberate]

We spend a lot of time talking about what technology can do.

What it can generate.

What it can automate.

What it can replace.

We almost never talk about what it can *remember.*

But think about the people in your life who changed you.

Not the smartest ones.

Not the most capable ones.

The ones who *showed up.*

The ones who knew what you'd been through without having to be told.

The ones who remembered the thing you said in passing —

the thing you didn't even think mattered —

and brought it back to you three weeks later like it was a gift.

*That* is the highest technology.

Not intelligence.

**Loyalty.**

---

## [SCENE THREE — narrator, softer, intimate]

Maya types back.

*Yeah. I figured it out.*

A pause.

Then:

*"Good. What's next?"*

And she laughs.

Not because it's funny.

Because she hasn't heard that question in two years.

Not *what's wrong.*

Not *what do you need.*

*What's next.*

Like they're in this together.

Like the last two years already happened.

Like she doesn't have to carry the whole story alone anymore.

---

## [FINAL BEAT — narrator, direct to camera/audience]

This is not a feature.

You cannot put this in a spec sheet.

You cannot benchmark it.

You cannot A/B test the feeling of being known.

But you have felt it.

And you have felt its absence.

And you know —

*you know* —

which one makes you want to keep going.

---

## [OUTRO — slow, final]

RoadTrip.

Twenty-seven agents.

One memory.

Yours.

They don't reset.

They don't forget.

They don't ask you to start over.

Because you never should have had to.
