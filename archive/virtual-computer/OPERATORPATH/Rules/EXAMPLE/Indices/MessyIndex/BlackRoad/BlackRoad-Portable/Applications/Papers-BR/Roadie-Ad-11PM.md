# Roadie — "11PM"
**Type**: Long-form Brand Ad — Full DI/Prose Interpretation
**Date**: April 10, 2026
**Product**: Roadie (roadie.blackroad.io)
**Style**: NSDA Dramatic/Prose Interpretation — multiple voices, full emotional arc
**Theme**: A parent who can't afford a tutor. A child who almost gave up. A tool that remembered.
**Core emotion**: The moment a child understands something for the first time — really understands it.

---

## [INTRODUCTION — narrator, quiet, direct]

Nobody tells you this part.

When you become a parent,

they tell you about the sleepless nights when they're newborns.

They don't tell you about the sleepless nights

when they're nine.

When they're sitting across from you at the kitchen table at 11pm

and they're crying

and you don't know the answer either

and you can't tell them that

because you're the parent

and you're supposed to know.

---

## [SCENE ONE — narrator, present, unhurried]

His name is Marcus.

He's nine years old.

He has a math test tomorrow.

He has been staring at the same problem for forty minutes.

His pencil is on the table.

His worksheet is crumpled at the corner

where he gripped it too hard

when he thought he had it

and then realized he didn't.

---

His dad, David, is next to him.

David works two jobs.

David hasn't slept more than six hours in four years.

David is looking at the worksheet

and he knows he knew this once.

Sixth grade. Maybe seventh.

But it's gone.

And Marcus is looking at him

the way children look at their parents

when they need them to have the answer —

that specific, terrible look —

and David

doesn't.

---

## [PAUSE.]

---

## [NARRATOR — lower now]

Private tutors cost forty dollars an hour.

Sixty. Eighty. More.

David did the math.

He always does the math.

It doesn't work.

---

They tried the apps.

The ones with the cartoon characters and the stars and the little celebration sounds.

Marcus did them for two weeks.

Got bored.

Fell behind anyway.

The app didn't know he'd been sick for three days in October

and missed the whole unit on fractions

that everything else is built on.

The app didn't know.

The app never knows.

The app just —

smiles —

and moves on.

---

## [VOICE SHIFT — an app. Cheerful. Hollow.]

*"Great job, Marcus! Ready for the next level?"*

---

## [NARRATOR. Flat. Tired.]

He was not ready for the next level.

He hadn't understood the last level.

But the app didn't ask.

The app never asks.

---

## [SCENE TWO — narrator shifts, something changes in the room]

David opens something different tonight.

He's heard about it.

He wasn't sure.

He types in: *Marcus has been struggling with fractions since October. He missed school. He doesn't understand why you flip the second one when you divide.*

And Roadie —

doesn't say *"Great! Let's get started!"*

Roadie says —

---

## [VOICE SHIFT — Roadie. Unhurried. Warm. Specific.]

*"Got it. A lot of kids hit that wall — the 'keep, change, flip' rule sounds like magic if no one explains the why. Can I show Marcus something? Not the rule. The reason behind the rule."*

---

## [NARRATOR. Barely above a whisper.]

David turns the laptop toward his son.

Marcus looks at the screen.

Suspicious.

He's been tricked by friendly screens before.

---

## [Roadie.]

*"Hey Marcus. Your dad told me you've been working really hard on this. I want to show you something — not a trick, not a rule to memorize. You're going to understand it. Like, actually understand it. You ready?"*

---

## [NARRATOR.]

Marcus looks at his dad.

David nods.

Marcus looks back at the screen.

*Yeah.*

*Okay.*

---

## [SCENE THREE — narrator, building slowly]

Twenty minutes later —

Marcus gets it.

Not *thinks* he gets it.

*Gets it.*

You can tell the difference.

It's in the posture.

The shoulders come down.

The pencil comes back up.

He works the next problem.

Then the next.

Then he looks up at his dad

and he says —

*"Oh. It's just because you're asking how many times it fits."*

---

David doesn't say anything for a second.

Because he is watching his son

understand something

for the first time.

Really understand it.

Not memorize it.

Not survive it.

*Own it.*

And he thinks —

*This.*

*This is why I work two jobs.*

*Not for the bills.*

*For this.*

---

## [LONG PAUSE.]

---

## [NARRATOR — building to the turn]

There's a myth we tell ourselves about education.

That it's about information.

That if you just give a child the right facts in the right order —

they'll learn.

But you've watched a child learn.

You know that's not how it works.

Learning happens in a specific moment.

A specific human moment.

When something clicks.

When the confusion lifts.

When a child looks up

and their eyes are different

than they were five minutes ago.

That moment doesn't come from an app.

It doesn't come from a textbook.

It comes from someone who was patient enough

to find the exact door

that was stuck

for *this* child —

not all children —

*this one* —

and open it.

---

## [SCENE FOUR — quiet, final]

Two weeks later.

Marcus gets his test back.

He got an 87.

He shows his dad in the car.

David stares at it longer than he needs to.

That night he types to Roadie:

*He got an 87.*

And Roadie says —

---

## [Roadie.]

*"I know. He told me this afternoon. He said he wants to try the next unit early. He wants to get ahead."*

---

## [NARRATOR. Still. Final.]

He wants to get ahead.

*He wants to get ahead.*

Three weeks ago he was crying at a kitchen table at 11pm

convinced he was stupid.

And now —

he wants to get ahead.

That is not an app.

That is not a feature.

That is a child

who was shown

that he is capable.

And a father

who didn't have eighty dollars an hour

but found something

that remembered his son —

*his specific son, with his specific gap, from his specific three days in October* —

and showed up anyway.

---

## [OUTRO. Slow. Direct.]

Every child deserves someone in their corner.

Not a cartoon. Not a level system. Not a streak.

Someone who remembers.

Someone who knows where they got stuck.

Someone who finds the door that's stuck for *them*

and opens it quietly

at 11pm

when everyone else is asleep.

---

Roadie.

For every kid who almost gave up.

For every parent who couldn't afford not to try.
